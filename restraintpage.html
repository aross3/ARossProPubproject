Restraints in JCPS often differ from 'arm hold or bear hug'

By Allison Ross

The use of restraints or seclusion in Jefferson County Public Schools has come into increasing focus this year after the revelation that the district had been severely underreporting the number of cases of such incidents to the state.

School districts across Kentucky self-report the number of times restraint and seclusion are used on their campuses, and in JCPS, schools are often left to police themselves about when it should be used and how.

The use of such measures has become a hot-button issue in recent years in school districts across the country, given evidence of potential abuse of the techniques as well as the possibility for physical and mental harm to children. The Courier-Journal has outlined instances where one child's legs were broken during a restraint and another where an elementary student vomited while being held up against a wall.

JCPS Superintendent Donna Hargens recently described restraints to the school board and public as "an arm hold to a bear hug." What restraints actually look like can vary, according to a Courier-Journal analysis of restraint and seclusion reports.

Restraint, seclusion used hundreds of times in one month

Of 323 incidents of restraint or seclusion the CJ reviewed from the month of February 2015, more than half included a seated or floor "cradle" restraint -- where a student was either held in a seated position on the floor or was brought to the floor in some position during the cradle restraint.

The CJ's analysis did not include a slew of incidents that school district officials found in a separate -- third -- database while attempting to find all the incidents of restraint and seclusion it is sending to the state.

Some restrained for nonviolent incidents

Kentucky regulations stipulate that physical restraint may only be used if a student's behavior "poses an imminent danger of physical harm to self or others."

A review of district data turned up dozens of incidents from February 2015 where students appeared to have been restrained or secluded for non-violent behaviors such as "refusal to follow directions," "class disruptions" and being out of their assigned area. A JCPS spokeswoman said that school staff may use restraints to force a student out of class if the student is causing "such a disruption that other students cannot learn."
